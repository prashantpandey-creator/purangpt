# RAG vs. Graph — the baseline benchmark

> **The bar this project is trying to beat.** Plain retrieval-augmented generation
> (RAG) is the obvious, cheap way to answer questions over a corpus: embed the
> chunks, retrieve the top-k by similarity, let the LLM read them. This document
> measures where that baseline *succeeds* and where it *structurally cannot*, so
> the knowledge-graph layer ([`recall.py`](recall.py)) has a concrete bar to clear.
> It is the empirical backing for the [`consciousness-over-RAG`] thesis in
> [`CONSCIOUSNESS_ROADMAP.md`](CONSCIOUSNESS_ROADMAP.md) and
> [`ARCHITECTURE.md`](ARCHITECTURE.md).
>
> **Reproduce it:** `venv/bin/python -m tools.rag_vs_graph_bench.check --json`
> (add `--quick` for a strided sample that runs in seconds). The numbers below are
> regenerated by that tool — do not hand-edit them; re-run and update.

## The thesis in one paragraph

RAG is a **strong baseline for two query classes** and **structurally incapable on
three**. It excels when the answer already exists as a contiguous passage (recite a
stotra) or as a description of one entity (who is Hanuman) — there, fetch-and-read
is the right tool and the graph is, at best, a faster tie. It **fails** when the
answer is a *relationship the corpus never states in one place*: a fact scattered
across chunks, a multi-hop chain assembled from many chapters, or a cross-text
identity linking a figure in one book to a figure in another. Those three classes
are exactly what the knowledge graph exists to win — and they are exactly where
similarity search has no mechanism, because you cannot *traverse a chain* or *merge
two identities* by measuring vector distance.

## The five query classes

| Class | What it asks | Corpus example |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| **PASSAGE** | Recite/return a span that exists verbatim | "Vishnu's 1000 names" → the Sahasranama, MBh 13.135, one contiguous stotra |
| **SINGLE-FACT** | Describe one entity | "Who is Hanuman?" |
| **SCATTERED** | A fact implied across chunks, never co-stated | "Who is Karna's father?" → Surya, but no single chunk says it cleanly |
| **MULTI-HOP** | A chain assembled from many places | "Shailendra Sharma's guru lineage up to Babaji" → Babaji→Lahiri→Tinkori→Satyacharan→Sharma |
| **CROSS-TEXT-ID** | Link a figure in one text to a figure in another | "Is Babaji the being who initiated Shankaracharya?" → the chandal legend |

## Honesty: this floor is a deliberate crude strawman

**The RAG floor is keyword term-overlap, NOT production pgvector semantic search.**
That matters, and hiding it would make the benchmark worthless:

- A real semantic RAG (the production `hybrid_search`) **would** improve PASSAGE and
  SINGLE-FACT — it finds the right passage by *meaning*, not word-count, so the
  crude floor's 0/5 understates a real RAG on the two easy classes. Read the top two
  rows as "RAG *can* do these," not "RAG fails these."
- But semantic RAG **would still fail SCATTERED, MULTI-HOP, and CROSS-TEXT-ID.**
  Vector similarity retrieves *similar text*; it has no operation to (a) infer a fact
  no chunk states, (b) walk a multi-step chain, or (c) decide two names are one
  entity across two books. Better embeddings retrieve better *passages* — they do
  not acquire *relational reasoning*. That is the graph's moat, and no amount of
  RAG tuning closes it.
- **Why the keyword floor scores 0/5 even on PASSAGE:** normalized term-overlap
  divides by query length, so short generic chunks (a thesaurus gloss, a one-line
  sutra) outrank dense narrative passages. This is a real, documented floor
  behavior — see the scope-bug note in `tools/rag_vs_graph_bench/README.md`
  (`--quick` originally sampled the alphabetical head and silently dropped
  `bhagavata`/`awakener`; fixed to stride). **Do not "fix" the floor to score
  higher — that defeats its purpose as the baseline.**

## The bar for future studies

1. **Match** RAG on **PASSAGE** and **SINGLE-FACT** — these are legitimately RAG's
   home turf; the graph need only tie (it does, ~150× faster). Do not over-build the
   graph to "win" a recitation; route those to RAG.
2. **Beat** RAG decisively on **SCATTERED**, **MULTI-HOP**, **CROSS-TEXT-ID** — this
   is the whole reason the graph exists. The lineage correction in
   [`GRAPH_CORRECTIONS.md`](GRAPH_CORRECTIONS.md) is a worked MULTI-HOP example: no
   chunk contains "Babaji→Lahiri→Tinkori→Satyacharan→Sharma"; only the graph's
   connected edges assemble it.
3. When evaluating a new retrieval idea, **upgrade the floor to real pgvector
   semantic RAG** before claiming a win on PASSAGE/SINGLE-FACT — the keyword floor
   here is honest about being a floor, not a fair semantic opponent on the easy
   classes.
