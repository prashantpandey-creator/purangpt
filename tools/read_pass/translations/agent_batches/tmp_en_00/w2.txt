Without looking at those present, who had hastily risen at his appearance, he sank down onto the divan standing at the back of the room.
Later I learned that he rarely sat directly against the walls — set against them, his aura became so dazzling that its white neon glow frightened newcomers. Sometimes strange transparent contours would appear above his head. Inconstant, flowing, they would take the conical form of a Shivalingam, or of a giant cobra with its hood spread wide. That day I saw nothing of the kind, perhaps because the overall impression was stranger than any luminous aura or silhouette. Despite the strength shining through his movements and gestures, the man on the couch looked very tired. The energy emanating from him was not quite human: heavy, merciless toward any falsity, devoid of compromise and of halftones. I thought to myself: this is the force of knowledge.

No one tried to break the heavy silence. Without removing his dark glasses, he was looking straight ahead with no expression. Having scanned me briefly, he was now looking "into" me. It became uncomfortable. Then the scanning turned into a cleansing. The feeling was as though someone had lifted the lid off my skull and was stirring the contents within, pouring in a disinfectant that burned out all the slag.

[Footnote on Rudraksha — the dried seeds of the sacred evergreen Elaeocarpus tree, used by Shaivites as prayer beads (mala) for the recitation of mantras and for worship. In Sanskrit the word means "the eye of Rudra" (Rudra being one of the forms of Lord Shiva).]

An unceremonious, invisible force was scraping away the mold of resentment and bitterness that had been collecting inside for long years, feeding insecurity and disappointment. Sensing this alien intrusion into the space of my mind, I froze. It was painful, but it was an indispensable condition of healing. What was required was the willingness to open the mind, the courage to step into the unknown. I had to remain there and receive the full dose of this strange therapy.

There was not the slightest sign of a descent of grace or of spiritual light. The presumed grace simply had nowhere to land, because my head was crammed with the accumulated rubbish of lived experiences — there was no room for anything new. Later I heard the term "Halahala", the poison of worldly existence, of samsara. We had all been poisoned by earthly existence, and here, in this place, the antidote was being administered.

[Footnote on Halahala — according to Hindu mythology, the deadly poison that was released from the Milk Ocean (the cosmic world ocean) during its churning. The Devas (gods) and the Asuras, the lesser deities also known as "demons" or "demigods" standing in opposition to the gods, churned the cosmic ocean to obtain the nectar of immortality, or amrita. Lord Shiva himself drank this poison to save all beings from its lethal vapors.]
[Footnote on Samsara — a fundamental belief of various Indian religions. The recurring cycle of deaths and rebirths. It is sometimes characterized by terms such as "transmigration", "karmic cycle", and "recurring reincarnations".]

Or perhaps these were my own fantasies, projections, and unfounded expectations? To feel uncertain in unfamiliar surroundings is entirely natural and normal — only this place was not normal, and it defied decoding.

…One after another, prostrating and bowing, people were leaving. I knelt and bowed, and my shawl slipped, covering my face. Beneath the dense woollen cloth all past events suddenly lost any meaning, as though a curtain had been lowered on a stage. When I rose from my knees, the curtain went up again — and another life began, as if I had awakened in a new and strange world. I said: "Happy upcoming New Year…". The man on the red divan responded distractedly, as an echo: "Yes, Happy New Year"…

…The audience was over, it was time to return to the hotel, but the state of inner stillness anchored me on the steps of the pond. It is hard to say how deep the waters were, how deep I had submerged into myself, and who in fact was submerging into what — but I did not want to move. Slow circles spread across the water, the duckweed trembled lightly at the brushing of dragonflies, dark silhouettes of fish moved beneath the surface, some transparent beings swarmed in the dark vaults of the lower tier of the palaces. There was not a soul around, although the noise of the city beyond the wall did not let up for a moment. The mind had become a non-mind; it no longer wished to calculate and classify, it wished to remain forever in this strange trance.

…Why had I wandered through India again and again? What did I want to find, what did I try to understand? The kaleidoscope of life here was too vivid to allow any clues to be picked out from its forever-shifting mosaics.

Of all the countless places, I had wanted to come here, to this Teacher, from the very day I saw his face on the cover of a yoga magazine almost a year before. I asked no one for the exact address of the ashram. I wanted to see and feel everything for myself, for the purity of the experiment and the objectivity of the first impression. And India, though at a rather ragged pace, was guiding me to my destination. It only remained to find out whether this was destiny or a chain of random coincidences.

In the hotel room it was cold. Tucking myself under a thin sleeping bag, I tried at least to make sense of the events of the last three weeks. They had been utterly chaotic, and the trip to Rajasthan had nearly done me in. The camel-and-jeep expedition itself, from Jaisalmer into the Thar desert, was not so very exhausting, but it was accompanied by a debilitating weakness from a banal case of food poisoning. One never knows where the ambush will be lying in wait. The innocent-looking banana cake from the "German" bakery turned out to be a treacherous treat.

Arriving in Delhi from Rajasthan, I spent the night in some filthy flop-house in Paharganj — and yet it was the best place for a swift transit through this huge, chaotic city resembling a noisy bazaar. The shabby hole bore the snobbish name "Kaiser's Castle". Had the Kaiser laid eyes on his castle, he would have had a stroke. Stalactites of grime, dust, and some greasy soot in the bathroom and under the bed looked so menacing that one had no wish even to speculate about the origin of the substance.

…December was almost frosty; the morning fog crept under one's clothes; out of it would emerge, and then again vanish, dimly distinguishable ghostly figures wrapped in shawls. At last the funnel of the city spat out a taxi onto the highway. After four unremarkable hours, the taxi finally turned off the busy main road and rattled along the gravel, maneuvering between the potholes. The fields were still covered with shreds of the same milky-white cold fog, but the landscape had changed. Now rural expanses dominated; civilization was left behind.

…The little town was packed with crowds; dense, endless streams of people parted before slowly moving cars. The approaching full moon seemed to be stirring up people and animals alike. Loudspeakers blared, cars honked, deafening music wailed. Here and there, right on the road, people were prostrating themselves in the dust; some were eating, others trading; mothers were nursing infants, many were singing. Scattered companies of dazed schoolchildren with badges pinned to their uniforms, sadhus in saffron robes, Gujarati

women with heavy silver anklets, monkeys and rickshaws — all tormented by an unceasing ecstasy — moved in unison, as though this grand production were directed by an invisible conductor. No, this place certainly resembled neither Rishikesh nor Goa. The comfort of the guest houses, cafés, and juice bars for European tourists had been left far behind, as though none of it had ever existed. I knew nothing of these traditions, of this town, of what lay ahead.

Apart from tea with biscuits and bananas, I had not been able to eat anything for four days. My head was empty, and even rang in a rather pleasant way. And yet that did not stop me from walking half of the parikrama — ten kilometres — in three and a half hours, in a state of complete amnesia. Even when a deranged beggar began kissing my bare feet, blessing me on the way, I did not flinch. I had neither the strength nor the desire to evade the respectful touch of his dirty fingers and lips. Now the waves of collective frenzy gave way to a measured rhythm of movement. There was not the slightest sign of weariness — only an intoxicating, filling lightness of being. Instead of enlightenment, some pure, childlike happiness descended upon me. This, no doubt, was the long-awaited Path, and a brief realization.

…I felt no "invitation" to stay, but I was certain that I would come back to see this indescribable man, this insane place that had pierced me to the depths of my soul with its harshness and its complete rejection of any unnecessary mental constructions. This land destroyed everything alien to it; the energy of the holy hill felt not at all "holy" — it was turning me inside out like a glove. The place demanded total submission and openness, and I wondered whether this yoga and this Teacher demanded the same.

Upon waking (or rather, coming to), I took out my notebook and pencil and drew the face with the dark glasses. The sketch came out well, but in the corners of the mouth something bitter lingered. And at the same time the expression of the face was calm and unperturbed. In it there was both the weight of Knowledge and absolute peace. Lying on the hard hotel bed, I did not yet know that I would spend many years in this country, that this yoga would become a part of me, and that the man whose portrait I had just drawn would become my Guide.

…The years that followed filled me with astonishment, admiration, at times with silent protests and doubts, with the truth about myself and about the world. Then came a gradual acceptance: of everything that had happened, and of what was still to come; of the outer and the inner, of the personal past and of the collective present. The Guru did not merely reveal to me the truth about the purpose of earthly existence and the reasons for my coming into this body. The yoga he taught showed all the invisible connections and the results of the personal karmic deeds that had led to the present incarnation. It was not simply shared and explained wisdom, but an inner knowledge that had become a part of me.

Nothing of what I heard or saw at the royal smashan during those years fit into the usual frames or settled into recognizable patterns. An unfamiliar reality bulged out of every form, like dough, breaking any vessel in which the elixir of common sense was bubbling.
All attempts to make sense of the non-eventfulness of what was taking place inevitably produced still greater confusion. The stubborn logical mind obstinately rejected everything not in its catalogue. At first it kept producing one and the same thought: who is the producer of this Bollywood show? Who has skilfully set up all these scenery pieces and chosen the wondrous accessories of the "Palace and Guru" stage?
