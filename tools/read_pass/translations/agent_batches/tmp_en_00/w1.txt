Meanwhile, spiritual shopping continues to lure customers with an ever-expanding variety of experiences to be lived. The abundance of spiritual goods on the shelves of the supermarket called "Enlightenment" has gone off the charts.
And yet the demand for still more unique, exclusive, and refined spiritual experiences keeps growing. Television preachers and evangelists, Zoom-yoga instructors, and YouTube tantric healers have invaded every possible niche, gaining ever greater popularity, fine-tuning sales algorithms, and targeting the right audience.
And still the search for true teachings, true knowledge, and true tradition does not cease. Persistent seekers try different techniques and methods, but the Path always begins with the search for a trustworthy Teacher, a reliable Guide, a real Guru. And this is the most difficult part of the Path.
What is the measure of Truth, and who may claim to explain it to others? Although we strive to know the nature of the Creator and His (or Her) Creation, an external impulse is required to advance along the path of self-realization, and such an impulse may be given by a Teacher. Yet what can give us confidence that a modern person, living and breathing the very same air as we do, is capable of offering answers to the eternal questions — what is Life, what is the meaning of human existence, where have we come from, where are we going?

A true spiritual mentor can be found only when at least a hint of an answer exists to a simple but fundamental question: who is a Guru?
The logical mind needs confirmation of the authenticity of everything — of love, of faith, of devotion. Today metaphysical conceptions are tested with sophisticated instruments: brain waves are measurable, the state of samadhi can be documented. But is it really possible to certify the authenticity of a spiritual teaching with a diploma, as in a college or a university? Or would it be better to set aside logic and open the heart and mind to a true Guru? Should this be a leap of faith or a pragmatic choice? In any case, intuitive or logical, the path of spiritual awakening is no easy one. It is very difficult to fathom the depths and the heights of spiritual Teachers, if one is fortunate enough to meet them at all. The difference in the frequency of brain-wave oscillations makes it harder for us to comprehend the state of those we consider enlightened. Since we cannot ourselves experience the higher states, we leave it to imagination and a limited perception to paint the portrait of a Guru.
The past century was generously blessed by the presence of spiritual Teachers. Inspiring and convincing, they awakened the minds and hearts of their contemporaries. The tumultuous development of Europe and North America at the beginning of the twentieth century demanded new ways of adapting to rapid change. The new times required a transformation of self-perception; people needed fresh ideas and methods of self-development. That is why the West so warmly received yoga.

On 31 May 1893 Swami Vivekananda sailed from Bombay to America to represent India and Hinduism at the Parliament of the World's Religions. Twenty-seven years later, on 19 September 1920, Yogananda set foot on American soil, arriving in the United States with a message about the universality of Kriya Yoga. Both yogis were pioneers in popularizing Eastern philosophy in the West, and their contribution to spiritual revival is truly immense.

[Footnote on Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902), Indian renunciate, monk, philosopher, disciple of Guru Ramakrishna — one of those who introduced the teachings of Vedanta and Yoga to the Western world. In 1893 he represented India at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago, where he delivered a speech on Hinduism.]

[Footnote on Yogananda Paramahansa (1893–1952), born in 1893 in Gorakhpur, Uttar Pradesh, as Mukunda Lal Ghosh, who received the spiritual name Swami Yogananda Giri in July 1915. World-famous Indian yogi and guru of Kriya Yoga. He lived for 32 years in the United States, disseminating the knowledge of yoga in the West. His celebrated "Autobiography of a Yogi" was published in 1946 and became a source of inspiration for millions around the world. Shri Shailendra Sharma, the hero of this biography, is the Fifth Guru of the same yogic lineage.]

[Footnote on Kriya — action, including creative action. In Tantra Kriya is regarded as action linked with knowledge that leads to perfection. Kriya Yoga is the yoga of action, since kriya is traditionally connected with deeds and with knowledge leading to perfection and self-realization. It consists of several levels of complex mudras, mantras, and pranayamas. These methods accelerate the development of mind and consciousness and bestow upon the practitioner a deep state of calm.]

Foreseeing the changes in the history of humanity, the masters of the parampara (the lineage of teachers) prepared Paramahansa Yogananda to present this yoga to the Western world.
Babaji said: "Kriya Yoga, the scientifically grounded technique of God-knowledge, will spread to all countries, helping to harmonize nations through the personal, transcendent perception of the Creator by the human being."
Mukunda Lal Ghosh (later known to the world as Yogananda) popularized the art of Kriya Yoga as the swiftest path to the Divine. It differed sharply from the propagation of religious beliefs. During his thirty years in the West, Yogananda initiated more than 100,000 Americans and Europeans into the practice of yoga. Awakening interest in the nature of spirituality and in the awareness of one's own spirit, offering methods of advanced self-knowledge, Yogananda brilliantly fulfilled his mission as an emissary from the East. In a certain sense, his appearance in the West can be compared to the return of the three Magi from the East — Melchior, Balthasar, and Caspar — who decided to test the now grown-up and rather matured, largely Christian society. When the three kings of the East came to bow down before the new Messiah Jesus, those Magi brought gifts laden with spiritual meaning. Gold was the symbol of earthly kingship; frankincense embodied the divine; myrrh, the embalming oil, was the emblem of death. Since then centuries have passed, and humanity needed new symbols, new stimuli, new meanings.

By means of Kriya Yoga, Yogananda endowed the West with methods of applying Will, Action, and Power in the spiritual quest. Thanks to him and to the teachers of this ancient tradition, the revolutionary idea of knowing God through knowing oneself began its triumphant ascent. It is impossible to enumerate here all those whom Yogananda's story inspired, but their accomplishments and victories vividly reflect the evolution of the human spirit. The life of Yogananda, as described in "Autobiography of a Yogi", changed the lives of millions. But what exactly impressed the readers? The persistence with which a young man sought hidden knowledge? The abundance of mystical omens and events, or the courage of an open heart and the perseverance in questioning the nature of universal love? Whatever the answer, one thing is clear: we, human beings, hunger for inspiration from those who walked the road to Truth before us.

Since 1946, after the publication of Yogananda's "Autobiography of a Yogi", no notable biographies of Masters of yoga of the same lineage have appeared. And yet the teaching has been preserved, the tradition continues to exist and to develop, because this teaching is universal, effective, and beyond the reach of time. Contemporaries interested in self-knowledge deserve to be acquainted with the continuation of this story. The life and work of Yogananda exerted a powerful influence on the destiny of the man who is the present spiritual mentor of the same lineage. Written for all who walk the thorny path of spiritual development, and for the merely curious, the authorized biography of the yogi, "The Awakener", tells of an incredible journey to the summit of spiritual attainment by a contemporary yogi and teacher capable of describing the indescribable and of conveying to his contemporaries authentic principles and knowledge.

The Fifth Guru in the line of Mahavatar Babaji, Shri Shailendra Sharma carries the genuine teaching of Kriya Yoga into the new century, guiding disciples on the path of yoga and of life. Not a worker of miracles nor a modern saint, but a realized yogi, he explores the connection with the supreme deity — Mother Earth. In his view, attaining the state of infinite unity with the universe is possible only when our own Spirit becomes our Guru, and our mind becomes the disciple of that Spirit.

[Footnote on Mahavatar Babaji — in Sanskrit "Maha" means "great", "Avatar" "the revered one", and Babaji "Father" or "Sage". The great mystical Guru of ancient Kriya Yoga; the founder of the Kriya Yoga tradition. He was the Teacher of Lahiri Mahasaya, whom he chose to spread the principles of Kriya Yoga throughout the world.]

The book you hold in your hands may seem unusual and even provocative (especially to those who still consider yoga a teaching about asanas). It is a story of the Spirit, of Time, of the road to self-knowledge and awakening. Of the Awakener.

The hero of this biography insisted on introducing himself to the reader in his own words: "Before you is the story of my life and of my personal observations of reality from the yogic point of view. It has nothing to do with castes, religions, or nationality. This is the story of the path of a son of the Earth, who values and admires Nature and makes sincere efforts to know his own nature. Without seeking to persuade anyone or to condemn the beliefs of others, I simply set down what I have observed and experienced. If you find these stories useful and entertaining — wonderful; if not — that is also fine."

The garden swept me along its pathways, between the trees, lured me onto the ramps of palaces, ensnared me in the traps of its furthest corners. At last, utterly disoriented, I drew near the doors of a great white house. On the steps at the entrance lay a sumptuous garland of crimson roses, and beside it stood a weeping young European woman. Why such a beautiful garland had been thrown away, and over what the young woman wept, was entirely beyond me. I knew almost nothing about the local traditions, the rules for visiting sacred places; the etiquette of ashrams was unfamiliar to me. It was clear only that here the standard European norms of conduct might not entirely apply.
